About

I'm a postdoctoral researcher at the Chair of Philosophy of Language and Mind at the Collège de France (Paris). My primary focus is on the structure of mental representation sharing and transmission in humans. This leads me to study various phenomena in social cognition, including human communication mechanisms, attitude attributions, interpretations of agreement and disagreement, joint attention, and inner speech.

Before joining the Chair of Philosophy of Language and Mind, I worked at the Conseil Scientifique de l'Éducation Nationale, where I coordinated the development of research-based resources to improve mathematics education in France (resources available here). This experience has intensified my interest in mathematical cognition. It has also made me aware of how crucial questions related to mental representation sharing and communication are in education. I am eager to continue working in this area.

I obtained my PhD in December 2022 from the Institut Jean Nicod, École Normale Supérieure (Paris). One of the main questions I have sought to answer is how we can explain successful communication. My hypothesis is that communication does not involve the replication of thought from speaker to hearer, but rather a form of coordination of thoughts. My goal has been to characterize and explain interpersonal coordination so construed. Part of my current work involves continuing to publish the results of this project.

Additionally, I'm currently involved in a project exploring the connections between inner speech, behavior, society, and health, in collaboration with cognitive scientists. Further details will be shared in due course!

Publications

Shareability of Thought and Frege’s Constraint: A Reply to Onofri
Inquiry: An Interdisciplinary Journal of Philosophy (2022)
Romain Bourdoncle
abstract | preprint | doi | cite

Onofri [Onofri, A. 2018. ‘The Publicity of Thought.’ Philosophical Quarterly 68 (272): 521–541.] proposes an individuation criterion for thoughts that purports to satisfy both shareability (the notion that different thinkers, or a single thinker at different times, can and generally do think type-identical thoughts) and Frege's constraint (according to which two thoughts are different if it is possible for a rational subject to endorse one while rejecting the other). I argue that his proposal fails to satisfy Frege's constraint. Then I propose a modification to Onofri's proposal to fix the problem.
